00 · Executive summary

Players want to do more with the skins they own. The obvious fix, open CS-style trading, is one Riot has deliberately rejected. The goal is therefore reframed: capture most of the player value while carrying none of the four risks.

note to selfThe whole trick is the reframe. Keep the upside and drop the four risks.

Valorant players want to recover value from unused purchases and to reliably find skins worth buying. Open player-to-player trading, however, breeds speculation and gambling, fuels scams and account theft, and cannibalizes Riot's direct-sale revenue. Those are the four reasons Riot said no.

The proposal is a Riot-operated, closed-loop Skin Vault built on four constraint-respecting mechanics. Buyback-to-Credit (the hero) lets Riot repurchase owned skins for non-cashable credit. A Usage-Weighted Store seeds one or two daily slots from the guns a player actually uses. Trial lets players test a skin for a match, capped tightly. Wishlist with Surprise Restock brings wanted skins back on a limited buy window.

No free-market pricing No cash-out No P2P asset transfer Buyback-to-Credit Usage-Weighted Store Trial Wishlist + Restock
01 · Problem statement

Players ask for trading, but trading is a solution, not the pain.

in the marginTrading is the workaround players reach for, not the pain itself.

When players say they want to trade, they are describing a workaround. The real underlying pains sit beneath it, and each one can be solved without opening a market.

  • PrimaryWasted spend and dead value. "I have bought skins I no longer use, for agents I dropped or weapons I don't run. That money feels dead, and I cannot recover any value from it."
  • DiscoveryThe store rarely shows what I want. "The daily rotation is random. Skins for the guns I actually play, or the one I have been eyeing, almost never appear, so I lose interest and never buy."
  • ConfidenceNo way to try before buying. "A knife or Vandal is expensive. I cannot tell if it feels right in-game until after I have paid, so I hesitate."

Invested Valorant players struggle to recover value from skins they own and to reliably find skins worth buying, because Riot's economy is strictly one-way and the store rotation is random. This drives buyer's remorse, lower future spend, and value leaking to gray-market account sales. It matters because skin revenue is Valorant's core monetization engine, and spend confidence drives it. The timing fits: the game is mature, players hold large aging inventories, and Riot has already shipped a closed-loop feature in gifting (Patch 10.08).

The backbone constraint

Why Riot says no to open trading

Any proposal that ignores these four documented reasons is dead on arrival. The whole design brief is to deliver the upside while structurally preventing all four of them.

Constraint 01

Direct-purchase model

Trading suits loot-box games that tune supply through randomized drops. Valorant has known quantities and set prices, so an aftermarket adds no design value. Joe Lee via Forbes, 2020

Constraint 02

Speculation & gambling

Open economies spawn third-party trading sites, price gouging, and skin-betting rings, the well-documented CS:GO problem. Esports Insider

Constraint 03

Account security & scams

Skin economies attract phishing, account theft, and bot-automated scam trades, all of which are direct player-safety costs. The Spike

Constraint 04

Stable revenue

No trading means every skin sale flows directly to Riot, whereas a peer-to-peer channel would cannibalize the store. The Spike

The reframed goal

Design a system that delivers value recovery, discovery, and liquidity while structurally preventing all four problems. Every feature below is scored against these constraints.

Value, liquidity, and better discovery, with no market, no cash-out, and nothing that truly changes hands.The brief, in one line.

03 · Success metrics

A North Star tied to revenue, not vanity engagement.

The entire thesis is that liquidity, discovery, and confidence increase willingness to keep spending. The North Star must therefore connect to Riot's revenue engine, guarded on all sides against cannibalization.

North Star Metric Spend Confidence, measured as Repeat Skin Purchase Rate (90-day)

The share of paying players who make a subsequent skin purchase within 90 days of using a Vault feature. If liquidity and discovery work, players keep buying with confidence.

04 · Solution & PRD

Vault, a Riot-operated, closed-loop economy.

Four mechanics, all closed-loop. Buyback is the hero; the other three drive discovery and purchase confidence. Each is scored against Riot's four constraints, shown on the right of every card. Gifting is left out because Riot already shipped it in Patch 10.08, useful precedent that a closed-loop model works.

01

Buyback-to-Credit Hero

The liquidity engine. Riot repurchases an owned skin for non-cashable store credit, and the skin is destroyed.

Riot repurchases an owned skin at a fixed, Riot-set credit rate (for example, 20 to 30% of the original VP price) paid as non-cashable store credit, and the skin is destroyed. It directly answers the primary pain of dead, unrecoverable spend.

  • Confirmation with irreversibility warning
  • Limited buybacks per period (anti-abuse)
  • Credit funds only part of a new purchase, never the full price
Riot's 4 constraints
Riot sets all prices, no market
Fixed rate means no speculation
Nothing transferable to steal
Credit recirculates in-store

Buyback guardrails

Buyback is the feature most likely to leak value, so it ships wrapped in rules that keep it revenue-accretive. It recovers dead spend without becoming a return window or a way to churn recent buys.

  • Long hold before eligibility. A skin must be owned for roughly 90 days before it can be sold back, so a purchase stays final instead of feeling like a return window.
  • Credit covers part of a purchase, not all of it. Buyback credit funds at most about half of any new skin, so every buyback still drives fresh cash spend.
  • New releases and active bundles are cash-only. A currently released bundle cannot be bought with buyback credit at all. Credit only applies once that skin cycles out of the bundle and returns to the normal store rotation.
  • Age-scaled rate. Genuinely old, retired skins earn a higher credit rate than recent buys, rewarding cleaning out a locker rather than unwinding a decision.
  • Eligibility scope is set by testing. Which tiers and skins qualify is not fixed at launch. It starts narrow and expands only if the cannibalization A/B shows total lifetime spend rising.
02

Usage-Weighted Store

Keep discovery alive, but make one or two daily slots reflect the guns a player actually uses.

Valorant's store shows four rotating skins a day, currently at random. This seeds one or two of the four from the player's most-used weapons, refreshed every alternate day, and keeps the rest on discovery rotation. The choice is deliberate: full personalization would trap players on one gun and kill exploration, so discovery slots stay.

  • Slots seeded from per-player weapon-usage data
  • No change to Riot-set pricing
Riot's 4 constraints
Direct-purchase preserved
No market, Riot-set prices
Nothing transferable
Revenue-positive by design
03

Trial

Try an expensive skin for real, but keep it scarce so it drives a purchase rather than replacing one.

A player can equip an expensive skin for a single match, capped at roughly two trials per month. Scarcity is the point: enough to close a hesitant purchase, but far too limited to replace buying.

  • Automatic expiry, no entitlement to keep
  • Clear "trial" tag while equipped, "Buy now" at end
Riot's 4 constraints
Nothing permanent transfers
No value moves
Expires, nothing to steal
Drives new purchases
04

Wishlist + Surprise Restock

Let players save skins they want, then bring them back as a surprise with a limited buy window.

Players wishlist the skins they want. Because rotation is random, a wanted skin can vanish for months and desire decays. Vault brings it back as a surprise, with no announced date, inside a limited 48-hour buy window. The surprise plus the countdown creates urgency ("buy now or wait for the next cycle") rather than the deferrable "I will just get it tomorrow" that kills a sale. This is not a loot box: the player chose the exact skin, only the timing is a surprise, so there is no randomized paid chance.

  • Restock interval is a tuned, tested variable
Riot's 4 constraints
Direct-purchase, Riot-set price
No randomized odds, not a loot box
Nothing transferable to steal
Revenue-positive by design

Explicitly out of scope, and why

Open P2P trading, a marketplace with player-set prices, real-money cash-out, and skin gambling or randomized cases are all excluded. Each one reintroduces one or more of the four risks, and keeping them off the table is what makes the rest of the design defensible.

05 · Unit economics

Where the money moves, and why it stays inside Riot.

Three of the four mechanics are near-costless upside. Only Buyback needs discipline, and it is bounded because credit is non-cashable and the skin is destroyed, so no real cash ever leaves Riot.

Needs discipline

Buyback-to-Credit

A skin bought at 1775 VP (about ₹1,200) buys back at 25% for roughly 444 VP of credit. Riot's cost is only a discount on a future purchase, so it is accretive as long as incremental future spend covers that discount.

skin = 1775 VP (~₹1,200)
credit @25% = ~444 VP (non-cashable)
accretive if: future spend ≥ credit discount
Pure upside

Usage-Weighted Store + Wishlist

Both are demand-capture, not new cost. Showing relevant skins and reviving wanted ones lifts conversion on inventory Riot already sells at full price. Incremental revenue equals the conversion lift multiplied by store traffic, and the only cost is engineering.

incremental rev = conv lift × traffic
cost ≈ build only
net = + upside
~Zero cost

Trial

Cost is near-zero, since a trial is just a toggle that expires, tightly capped at about two per month. The upside is trial-to-purchase conversion on high-price items, where the barrier is uncertainty rather than desire.

cost ≈ 0 (capped toggle)
upside = trial→buy conversion
on high-price items

Business case summary

  • Buyback turns dead inventory into recirculating credit and spend confidence.
  • Store, Wishlist, and Trial lift conversion on skins Riot already sells, at build cost only.
  • Together they reclaim value and demand leaking to a random store and gray-market sales, where Riot earns ₹0 today.

07 · Phased rollout

Ship the safe wins first, and gate the risky one on evidence.

Every phase has a kill or scale gate tied to guardrail metrics, meaning direct revenue stays flat or rises and fraud stays at or below baseline.

0
Pre-launch

Research & guardrails

Instrument the spend-confidence baseline, fraud baseline, and direct-revenue-per-user baseline. Conduct a legal review of gambling and real-money regulations by region.

◆ Gate: baselines instrumented and legal cleared
1
Lowest risk · no economic transfer

Usage-Weighted Store + Wishlist

Launch usage-weighted store slots and wishlist with surprise restock to one region. Confirm the surprise-and-countdown mechanic is understood and not perceived as a random paid draw.

◆ Gate: conversion ↑ and no gambling-perception flags in legal review
2
Gated on experiment

Buyback-to-Credit

Run an A/B test in one region at a fixed 25% non-cashable rate, excluding limited and exclusive skins initially. The primary read is whether total lifetime spend rises versus the control group.

◆ Gate: total lifetime spend ↑ vs control
3
Conversion driver

Trial

Add capped trial (one match, about two per month) on high-price items and measure the trial-to-purchase lift on those SKUs.

◆ Gate: measurable trial→purchase lift
4
Scale

Global rollout + tuning

Roll winners globally, tune the buyback rate by segment, and expand the eligible catalog carefully.

◆ Gate: guardrails hold at global scale
08 · Risks & mitigations

Six risks, each with a structural answer.

Cannibalization

Buyback could reduce cash sales. Mitigate: a low fixed non-cashable rate, the skin destroyed on buyback, and an A/B gate on total lifetime spend before scaling.

Surprise-restock FOMO optics

A surprise return with a 48-hour timer can look like manipulative urgency. Mitigate: the player chose the exact skin and pays a fixed price, so there is no randomized paid draw, unlike a loot box. Keep the timing pressure honest and legally reviewed per region.

Buyback & trial abuse

Mitigate: buyback caps per period, tight trial limits (one match, about two per month), and exclusion of limited or exclusive skins from buyback.

Discovery narrowing

Usage-weighted slots could trap players on one gun. Mitigate: cap personalization at one or two of four slots and keep the rest on discovery rotation, so players still see new skins.

Precedent / slippery slope

Some may fear this leads to open trading. Mitigate: keep it explicitly closed-loop and communicate clearly that P2P trading stays off the table, and why.

Support load

Irreversibility confusion on buyback. Mitigate: strong confirmation UX and a short refund window on buyback.

09 · Open questions

What I'd want to resolve next.

  1. What wishlist restock interval maximizes conversion without training players to simply wait for the next cycle? Too frequent kills scarcity, too rare kills interest. This is the critical variable to A/B test.

  2. What buyback rate maximizes total lifetime spend without training players to under-buy? This needs an experiment.

  3. How many of the four daily store slots should be usage-weighted before discovery suffers? One, or two?

  4. Where should buyback eligibility stop? Scope starts narrow and expands only if the cannibalization A/B shows total lifetime spend rising, so which tiers open up, and in what order, is a gated decision, not a launch assumption.

  5. Should buyback credit expire, which drives urgency, or persist, which builds goodwill? This trade-off is worth testing.

  6. Given regional legal variance in gambling and consumer-protection law, which markets should launch first?
